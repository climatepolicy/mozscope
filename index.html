Mozambique Scoping Update

  • Mozambique uses a lot of land for energy and a little for food (Physical)

    Land can be used for food, for fuel, or for ecosystem services. In Mozambique, most land is being used for low-value fuel, and the land being used for food is used unproductively. This leads to poverty, as the country has little to sell, and food insecurity, as the country has little to eat.

    • Today, most land is not being used to its potential

      • A huge amount of land is used for charcoal and firewood.

      • Most agricultural land is used for local production

        • Local crops include cassava, poultry, etc.

        • A small amount of land is dedicated to export agriculture

        • Some land is protected, but effectiveness is hard to discern

          • A few parks and reserves

          • A few voluntary carbon offsets projects

    • Mozambique's high-quality land could produce more

      This section is about physical potential. Policy and non-policy barriers prevent the interventions here from taking place, and those will be discussed in the policy/fiscal section.

      • Existing waste can be converted to energy

        Sugar mills are producing energy, but could produce more and export it to the grid.

      • Energy can be obtained from other sources

        Using land for energy currently makes sense economically, as the investment needed for higher-value uses is constrained.

        • New gas reserves mean natural gas will be plentiful.

        • Solar energy may make sense off-grid

        • Ethanol has potential, but may be outcompeted by gas

      • High value foods currently imported can be produced locally

        • Cassava
        • Wheat
        • Maize
        • Rice
        • Poultry

      • Exports of high value food can be increased.

        • Sugar
        • Tropical fruits
