Mozambique Scoping Update

Overview

This paper will identify key priorities moving forward as a result of the scoping done in the final quarter of 2013. To that end, it will have two key parts:

  • An overview of how land is currently used

  • A discussion of key near-term opportunities for investments that would reduce emissions while earning positive returns. Further investigation of these areas is warranted.

Overall, land in Mozambique is used rather unproductively, and natural ecosystems are poorly protected.

Mozambique uses a lot of land for energy and a little for food (Physical)

Land can be used for food, for fuel, or for ecosystem services. In Mozambique, most land is being used for low-value fuel, and the land being used for food is used unproductively. This leads to poverty, as the country has little to sell, and food insecurity, as the country has little to eat.

Today, most land is not being used to its potential

Low-productivity subsistence agriculture and uncultivated Miombo woodlands increasingly stripped for charcoal production dominate.

A huge amount of land is used for charcoal and firewood.

Currently, the charcoal sector employs a large segment of the population, and constitutes the majority of cooking fuel. This is leading to deforestation and health problems. Further, it isn't even particularly cheap.

Most agricultural land is used for local production

  • Local crops include cassava, poultry, etc.

  • A small amount of land is dedicated to export agriculture

  • Some land is protected, but effectiveness is hard to discern

    • few parks and reserves

    • few voluntary carbon offsets projects

Mozambique's high-quality land could produce more

This section is about physical potential. Policy and non-policy barriers prevent the interventions here from taking place, and those will be discussed in the policy/fiscal section.

Existing waste can be converted to energy

Sugar mills are producing energy, but could produce more and export it to the grid.

Energy can be obtained from other sources

Using land for energy currently makes sense economically, as the investment needed for higher-value uses is constrained.

  • New gas reserves mean natural gas will be plentiful.

  • Solar energy may make sense off-grid

  • Ethanol has potential, but may be outcompeted by gas

  • High value foods currently imported can be produced locally

    • Cassava
    • Wheat
    • Maize
    • Rice
    • Poultry

  • Exports of high value food can be increased.

    • Sugar
    • Tropical fruits

Unlocking Productive Land use in Mozambique: CPI priorities moving forward [PRELIMINARY]

CPI efforts should focus on sectors with near-term potential for bankable, emissions-reducing investment.

Providing information on how to properly invest in agriculture.

Investors must undertake two processes in parallel. Misunderstanding of this unique system in the past has led investors to lose land rights or be chased out of country by local populations.

MINAG Approval

  • Start by working with the local government

  • Local government will engage provincial/national if needed

    • for concessions over 10,000 ha

    • where their expertise is insufficient

CPI Approval (centro de promoção de investimento)

  • Must approve all foreign investments

  • This process is easier than the above.

Highlight the need for improved infrastructure

Transit of goods is too slow to react to markets and keep things fresh. This is a known issue, and will take time

Electricity grid is lacking, and off-grid renewables policy is poorly developed

Agricultural projects currently rely on diesel generators for power off-grid, which is both expensive and environmentally unfriendly. Solar can be cheaper, create more jobs, and is better for the environment.

For now, focus on areas where infrastructure is improving, or focus on integrated local markets

  • Beira Corridor is currently undergoing significant upgrades

  • Gas investment near Pemba is leading to rapid infrastructure buildout.

Locally consolidated value chains can be profitable even absent infrastructure.

For example, chicken feed (soy + maize) and chickens near urban centers.

Cogeneration from sugar sector
